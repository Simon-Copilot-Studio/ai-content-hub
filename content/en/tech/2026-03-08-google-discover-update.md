When Google announced its February 2026 Discover Core Update on February 6th, most publishers braced for the familiar turbulence of a broad core algorithm refresh. What they didn't expect was the unprecedented scope: this was Google's **first core update explicitly targeting Google Discover** as a distinct surface — separate from traditional Search rankings — and the 22-day rollout left traffic analytics dashboards swinging by as much as ±40% for major media outlets.

## What Makes This Update Different

Every major core update since 2018 has applied algorithm changes broadly across Google Search, with Discover traffic treated as a downstream effect. The February 2026 update broke that pattern.

According to Google's Search Liaison team, this update "focuses specifically on how content surfaces in the Discover feed, including how we evaluate topical authority, visual asset quality, and content freshness signals for feed-based environments." In plain language: the signals that make an article rank well in Search are no longer identical to the signals that surface it in Discover.

The **22-day rollout** — unusually long by Google's standards — was itself a signal that the system changes were substantial. Google's typical core updates complete within 1–2 weeks. The extended timeline suggests significant infrastructure changes to how Discover's recommendation engine evaluates and re-ranks content in real time.

## Key Ranking Signals That Shifted

Early analysis from SEO firms including Semrush, Ahrefs, and Search Engine Roundtable points to several newly weighted factors in Discover:

**1. Visual Asset Diversity**
Publishers with multiple high-resolution images per article (minimum 1,200px wide) and at least one embedded video saw measurably stronger Discover impressions. Text-heavy articles with a single stock photo suffered notable drops.

**2. Topical Authority Clusters**
Google appears to be weighting *site-level topical clusters* more heavily for Discover than for Search. Publishers who publish consistently within a defined niche — rather than broad general interest content — showed stronger resilience through the update.

**3. Engagement Velocity**
Early engagement signals (click-through rate in the first 2–4 hours after publication) appear to carry more weight in Discover's feed ranking. This favors publishers with loyal newsletter audiences and social distribution pipelines that can drive immediate traffic.

**4. AI-Generated Content Flags**
Several publishers reported sharp drops in Discover traffic for articles that Google's systems apparently flagged as low-originality AI-generated content. Human editorial voice, first-person perspectives, and cited original sources became more critical than ever.

## AI Overviews: Reshaping the Discover Landscape

The February update also coincided with the broader rollout of **AI Overviews in Google Discover** — summarized, AI-generated cards appearing above traditional article links in the feed. For users, this is convenient. For publishers, it's complicated.

Traffic from Discover to individual articles dropped for some categories (breaking news, how-to guides) while increasing for others (opinion, long-form analysis, original reporting). The pattern mirrors what happened to Search traffic when AI Overviews launched in 2024: commoditized information loses; original perspective wins.

## Practical Steps for Publishers

1. **Audit your image library**: Every article should have at least 2–3 high-resolution images. Add video embeds where editorially appropriate.
2. **Build topical silos**: If you cover tech broadly, create clearly delineated sections (AI, hardware, security) with consistent internal linking.
3. **Invest in distribution speed**: Build or grow an email list and social channels that can drive early engagement within hours of publication.
4. **Prioritize original sourcing**: Cite data, conduct interviews, add first-person perspectives that AI summarization cannot replicate.
5. **Monitor Search Console's Discover report**: Separate your Discover and Search performance data. The metrics that matter now diverge meaningfully.

The February 2026 Discover Core Update is a watershed moment. Google has effectively declared Discover a first-class content surface with its own algorithmic logic. Publishers who adapt their content strategy accordingly will find new growth opportunities — those who don't may find their Discover traffic permanently diminished.
