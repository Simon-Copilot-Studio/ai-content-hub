Tangyuan (湯圓) are soft, chewy glutinous rice balls filled with sweet pastes — black sesame, peanut, or matcha red bean. In Taiwan, they're the essential food of the Lantern Festival, eaten to celebrate family reunion and the first full moon of the lunar new year.

This recipe teaches you three fillings and one perfectly balanced dough, so you can make restaurant-quality tangyuan at home.

## Why Tangyuan Matter

The Lantern Festival falls on the 15th day of the first lunar month, marking the end of Chinese New Year celebrations. Eating tangyuan on this night isn't just tradition — it's an expression of hope. The round shape symbolizes **reunion** (團圓) and **completeness**, echoing the full moon overhead.

In many Taiwanese families, making tangyuan is a group activity. Grandparents, parents, and children gather around the table, each rolling their own little spheres of dough. The act of making them together *is* the reunion they symbolize.

## The Dough: Ratio Is Everything

Many people fail at tangyuan not because of technique, but because of water temperature. This single variable determines everything.

**The formula:**
- 150g glutinous rice flour
- 115–120ml warm water at **55°C (131°F)**
- 10g sugar (optional)

### Why 55°C?

Too hot (above 70°C) and the starch gelatinizes prematurely — the dough becomes sticky and unworkable. Too cool (below 45°C) and the flour won't hydrate properly, leading to dry, crumbly dough that cracks when you try to wrap it.

At 55°C, the flour partially activates, creating a dough that's pliable, smooth, and forgiving. After kneading, it should feel like an earlobe — soft but not sticky.

**Rest the dough 15 minutes** under plastic wrap. This lets the flour fully absorb the water, making it far easier to shape without cracking.

## Three Classic Fillings

### 1. Black Sesame (The Classic)

| Ingredient | Amount |
|-----------|--------|
| Black sesame powder | 50g |
| Lard or butter | 25g |
| Powdered sugar | 30g |

Mix softened lard with powdered sugar, then fold in sesame powder until it holds together. Roll into 8g balls and refrigerate 20 minutes.

**Why lard?** It's traditional and produces the most fragrant, smooth filling. Unsalted butter is an excellent substitute.

### 2. Peanut (Crunchy Comfort)

| Ingredient | Amount |
|-----------|--------|
| Peanut powder | 50g |
| Crushed roasted peanuts | 20g |
| Lard or butter | 25g |
| Powdered sugar | 20g |

Same method as sesame. The crushed peanuts add texture — when you bite into the tangyuan, you get both creamy paste and satisfying crunch.

### 3. Matcha Red Bean (Modern Twist)

| Ingredient | Amount |
|-----------|--------|
| Sweet red bean paste | 100g |
| Matcha powder | 5g |
| Butter | 20g |

Melt butter, whisk in matcha until smooth, then fold into red bean paste. This filling has more moisture, so **freeze it for 30 minutes** before wrapping — it holds its shape much better when cold.

## Wrapping: The 15:8 Ratio

The ideal ratio is **15g dough to 8g filling**. This gives you a wrapper thick enough to stay intact during cooking but thin enough to let the filling shine.

### How to Wrap

1. Roll 15g of dough into a ball
2. Press your thumb into the center, creating a bowl shape
3. Pinch the edges thin (2–3mm)
4. Place the filling ball inside
5. Use the "tiger mouth" of your hand (between thumb and index finger) to push the dough up and around the filling
6. Pinch closed, then gently roll smooth between your palms

**Beginner tip:** If your dough cracks while wrapping, it's too dry. Wet your hands slightly and knead a few more times.

## Cooking: The Cold Water Technique

This traditional method ensures the tangyuan cook evenly — firm on the outside, molten on the inside.

1. **Bring a large pot of water to a rolling boil** — use plenty of water so tangyuan don't crowd
2. **Gently slide tangyuan in** — don't drop from height
3. **When they float**, add half a cup of cold water
4. **Wait for the second float**, add cold water again
5. **After the third float**, cook **1 more minute**, then remove with a slotted spoon

The cold water additions slow the cooking, letting heat penetrate to the center without overcooking the outside. This is the same logic behind adding cold water when cooking dumplings.

## Serving Suggestions

### Classic: Ginger Sugar Syrup
Simmer sliced ginger, rock sugar, and water for 15 minutes. Ladle over cooked tangyuan. Add a pinch of osmanthus flowers for an old-Taipei touch.

### Simple: Sweet Soup
Just sugar water with a few goji berries and red dates — clean and light.

### Modern: Shaved Ice
In summer, serve tangyuan over shaved ice with condensed milk, taro balls, and red bean. A popular Taiwanese night market dessert.

## Storage

| Method | Duration | Notes |
|--------|----------|-------|
| Room temperature | 4 hours | Best eaten fresh |
| Refrigerator | 2 days | May harden; re-boil briefly |
| Freezer | 1 month | Freeze on tray first, then bag |

## More Than a Recipe

Learning to make tangyuan is learning to hold a wish in your hands — for togetherness, for sweetness, for another year with the people you love.

In Taiwan, the act of rolling these little balls isn't just cooking. It's a quiet declaration: *I'm here, and I'm grateful we're together.*

That's worth learning to get the water temperature right.
