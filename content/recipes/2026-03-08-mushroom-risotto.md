Risotto intimidates people unnecessarily. Yes, it requires attention — 20 minutes of stirring — but it's not difficult. It's meditative. And the reward is one of the most luxurious dishes in Italian cuisine, made from the humblest of ingredients.

## The Science of Creamy Risotto

Arborio rice is different from regular long-grain rice. Its short, plump grains contain a higher proportion of amylopectin starch, which dissolves into the surrounding liquid when agitated. Each stir releases a tiny bit of starch, and over 18-20 minutes, those tiny bits accumulate into a creamy sauce that requires no cream at all.

## The Five Critical Steps

### 1. Toast the Rice

Before any liquid touches the rice, toast it in butter for 2-3 minutes. You'll hear a faint crackling sound and see the edges turn translucent. This step develops a nutty flavor and creates a slight barrier on the outside of each grain, helping them maintain their shape.

### 2. Deglaze with Wine

The wine serves two purposes: it adds acidity and complexity, and the alcohol helps extract flavor compounds from the aromatics. Use something you'd actually drink — cheap wine makes cheap-tasting risotto.

### 3. Warm Broth Only

Cold broth shocks the rice and slows cooking unevenly. Keep it warm in a separate saucepan, and add it one ladleful at a time. This is the part that requires patience.

### 4. Stir (But Not Constantly)

Contrary to popular belief, you don't need to stir without stopping. Stir every 30-60 seconds — enough to release starch and prevent sticking, but not so much that you break the grains.

### 5. The Mantecatura

This is the final step that separates good risotto from great: remove from heat, add cold butter and Parmesan, and stir vigorously. The cold fat emulsifies into the hot rice, creating an incredibly silky, glossy finish.

## Mushroom Selection

Mix varieties for complexity:
- **Cremini** — earthy baseline flavor
- **Shiitake** — intense umami, meaty texture
- **Button** — mild and tender
- **Dried porcini** — rehydrate in the broth for concentrated mushroom flavor (game-changer)

Sauté mushrooms separately at high heat until deeply golden. This Maillard reaction creates flavors that simmering directly in the risotto cannot achieve.
